Overview of Project

In today’s highly-technological world, the internet has immense power to change the world for the better. Or so we think. There are those who support the internet as a tool for social and political change, and those who are critics.

Evgeny Morozov argues that we have a “naïve belief in the emancipatory nature of online communication that rests on a stubborn refusal to acknowledge its downside” (Morozov, p. xiii). One of Morozov’s main arguments is cyber-utopianism: we assume that the internet favors the oppressed rather than the oppressor, but in actuality that doesn’t always hold. An offshoot of this is the Google Doctrine: that the ‘liberating power of technology’ is overstated and doesn’t help the fight for democracy or human rights. In a nutshell, Morozov argues the net delusion: that we believe too much in the power of technology to democratize our world and help the oppressed. It’s important that we don’t focus on the internet but rather local action.

On the flip side, Muzzamil Hussain believes in the power of technology to fuel Fourth Wave Democracy; the internet’s power to connect people globally and anonymously gives way to real-world change. Digital activism empowers communities to raise awareness online, which transfers directly to demonstration and change in the ‘real world.’

Our site explores topics and arguments of both Morozov and Hussain, and how their topics play out in the real world. We hope you find it interesting and informative, and learn something new.
